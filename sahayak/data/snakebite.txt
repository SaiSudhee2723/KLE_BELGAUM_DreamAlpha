SNAKE BITE — ICMR / MoHFW India Emergency Guidelines

OVERVIEW
India: 50,000+ snakebite deaths annually. Venomous species: Spectacled Cobra (Naja naja), Russell's Viper (Daboia russelii), Common Krait (Bungarus caeruleus), Saw-scaled Viper (Echis carinatus) — "Big Four".

TYPES OF VENOM
Neurotoxic (Krait, Cobra): Affects nerves — drooping eyelids, blurred vision, difficulty swallowing, respiratory paralysis. Fang marks may be minimal.
Cytotoxic/Haemotoxic (Viper): Local tissue destruction + coagulopathy — massive swelling, bleeding from wound, systemic bleeding, blood in urine, DIC.
Myotoxic (Sea snake): Muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis), dark urine.

IMMEDIATE FIRST AID (CRITICAL)
1. Immobilise the bitten limb — keep still and at or below heart level.
2. Remove rings, bangles, tight clothing near bite site.
3. Mark the edge of swelling with pen + time (to track progression).
4. Transport IMMEDIATELY to hospital — do not delay.
5. Do NOT: incise/cut the wound, suck venom, apply tourniquet, apply ice/heat, give oral medications.
6. Try to note snake description (colour, pattern, size) — do not try to catch snake.
7. Keep patient calm — reduce heart rate to slow venom spread.

HOSPITAL TREATMENT
Anti-snake venom (ASV): polyvalent ASV — ONLY effective treatment. Administer IV.
Test dose (0.1 mL SC/ID) then full dose IV over 30–60 min.
10 vials initial dose for systemic envenomation; repeat if no improvement at 1 hour.
Premedication with Adrenaline 0.25 mg SC + Promethazine IV to prevent ASV anaphylaxis.
Neostigmine (+ Atropine): for neurotoxic envenomation — reversal of paralysis.
Ventilator support: for respiratory paralysis.

SIGNS OF SYSTEMIC ENVENOMATION (requires ASV)
Haemotoxic: Non-clotting blood (20-minute whole blood clotting test — WBCT20 > 20 min), spontaneous bleeding from gums/wound/IV site, haematuria.
Neurotoxic: Ptosis (drooping eyelid), diplopia, difficulty swallowing/breathing, ascending paralysis.
Cytotoxic: Extensive local necrosis, spreading beyond bite site.

RED FLAGS (ALL snakebite patients must go to hospital)
- Any neurotoxic signs → ventilation risk — do not leave alone
- Respiratory distress → immediate intubation at hospital
- Signs of systemic bleeding → ASV urgently
- Renal failure (no urine output) — dialysis may be needed
- Anaphylaxis after ASV → Adrenaline immediately

ASHA ACTION
1. First aid as above — immobilise + immediate transport.
2. Reassure patient (panic increases heart rate and venom spread).
3. Call 108 ambulance.
4. Do NOT give any home remedies, tourniquets, or herbal medicines.
5. All snakebite victims go to hospital regardless of symptoms — krait bite may be painless but lethal.
6. Record time of bite, snake description, progression of swelling.
7. Community: educate on sleeping on raised beds/cots in endemic areas, torch at night, boots in fields.
