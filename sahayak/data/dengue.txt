DENGUE FEVER — WHO / NVBDCP Clinical Guidelines

CLINICAL FEATURES
Symptoms: Sudden high fever (39–40°C), severe headache, retro-orbital pain, myalgia, arthralgia ("breakbone fever"), skin rash (maculopapular, appears day 3–5), nausea/vomiting.
Dengue Haemorrhagic Fever (DHF): bleeding (petechiae, ecchymosis, epistaxis, gum bleeding), plasma leakage, thrombocytopenia.
Dengue Shock Syndrome: severe plasma leakage → hypotension, narrow pulse pressure (< 20 mmHg), rapid weak pulse.

WHO DENGUE CLASSIFICATION
Group A: No warning signs → manage at home.
Group B: Warning signs or co-morbidities → hospitalise.
Group C: Severe dengue → emergency treatment.

WARNING SIGNS (admit immediately)
- Abdominal pain or tenderness
- Persistent vomiting
- Clinical fluid accumulation (ascites, pleural effusion)
- Mucosal bleeding
- Lethargy, restlessness
- Liver enlargement > 2 cm
- Rapid increase in haematocrit with drop in platelets

DIAGNOSIS
NS1 antigen (day 1–5), IgM/IgG antibodies (from day 5).
CBC: thrombocytopenia (platelets < 1,00,000), raised haematocrit (> 20% rise = plasma leak).
Tourniquet test: ≥ 10 petechiae per sq inch = positive.

TREATMENT
No specific antiviral. Supportive care.
Adequate oral hydration (ORS, coconut water, juices).
Paracetamol for fever (500 mg q6h). AVOID aspirin/NSAIDs (risk of bleeding).
Platelet transfusion only if < 10,000 or active bleeding.
IV fluid for DHF — cautious isotonic crystalloid, avoid over-hydration.

RED FLAGS (Emergency referral)
- Platelets < 50,000 with bleeding
- Shock (cold extremities, hypotension, altered consciousness)
- Respiratory distress
- Liver failure, encephalopathy
- Haematocrit rise > 20% with platelet drop

ASHA ACTION
1. Suspect dengue: fever ≥ 3 days + body pain + rash.
2. Advise paracetamol only (not aspirin/ibuprofen).
3. Ensure adequate oral fluids.
4. Refer to PHC for NS1/CBC test.
5. Warn: warning signs → go to hospital immediately.
6. Eliminate Aedes mosquito breeding (stagnant water in containers, tyres, pots).
7. Report cluster to BMO.
